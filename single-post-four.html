The Need for Adequate Public Latrines in Liberia

Author: Fayiah S. Tamba

Owing to the philosophy, “population brings growth,” I have observed that development via population growth is not without a challenge. In Liberia, limited public toilet facilities pose a serious challenge to the health of the population and the development of the country.

I suppose that low budgetary support and overpopulation are the causes of this problem. Since 2015 Liberia's population started rapidly growing and is now estimated at 5.06million (World Population Review, 2020). JMP 2017 report reveals, "Less than 20% of Liberians do not have access to privately-owned latrines. About 42% of the population practice open defecation."

The government of Liberia and its development partners had been committed to ensuring environmental health and sustainability by implementing a series of measures towards achieving the MDGs (#7) and SDGs (#6). However, there are still, unresolved public health issues confronting the health sector, with which lack of adequate public toilets is paramount.

According to All Africa Liberia (December 2018), some influential government officials promised Liberians the construction of thousands of public toilet facilities in Monrovia but the promise has not been fulfilled. According to the Water and Sanitation Program (2012), Liberia had lost 17. 5 million US Dollars annually due to poor sanitation as citizens are being affected by waterborne diseases and infections.

This expenditure claimed a high budgetary allocation and depleted Government saving. Contrary to the saying, "prevention is better than cure," it is obvious that the Liberian Government spends more on curative health than preventive care. Liberia has a fewer on-street toilets to accommodate travelers and passers-by.

Almost all of the on-street latrines are privately-owned. Attendants charge high service fees. These privately-owned public latrines are built with makeshift structures, muds, and concrete blocks but have limited rooms to accommodate 3-5 people at once. Notwithstanding, the Government of Liberia has built only a few off-street public toilets in some urban and rural communities.

Many people do not have private latrines in their households. They use buckets as toilets and empty them at night. This problem threatens and compromises the health of the public, particularly residents of slum communities, of which women and girls are mostly affected with chronic infections.

The provision of public latrines is a significant aspect of ensuring environmental sustainability and achieving Sustainable Development Goals #3 (Good Health and Well-being) and #6 (Clean Water and Sanitation). Increasing the adequacy of public toilets implement a sustainable sanitation program across Liberia for the creation of a healthy environment.

To mitigate the threat to a healthy environment posed by inadequate public toilet facilities across Liberia, particularly in urban communities, key ministries and agencies must implement these strategies:

1. Increase the number of public toilets in slum communities and other former settlements for the prevention of waterborne diseases and infections and eradication of open defecation.

2. Prioritize the need for budgetary supports to step-up its intervention by increasing access to public toilet facilities across the country.

3. Collaborate with private managers or NGOs to have interest and invest in the construction of on-street toilet facilities.

4. Create a maintenance team to manage the operation and usage of the public toilet facilities to ensure environmental sustainability and prevent ushers from waterborne diseases and infections.

5. Plant a signpost at intersections or on the roadsides to help travelers find the public toilets. Place the signpost blocks or meters away from the toilets' destination to enable people from other regions or localities to access the public toilet.

In conclusion, I would like to encourage private investors to take advantage of constructing on-street public toilets to ease the burden many Liberians are now facing. I think if the Government of Liberia has limited resources to prioritize this project, it would be welcoming for interested business individuals to study the case and remedy the situation.

I assure whoever is interested will generate money from this project. Many Liberians cannot even access the limited existing toilet facilities constructed in communities. They would prefer using the on-street public toilet facilities, as long as they are clean and spacious.
